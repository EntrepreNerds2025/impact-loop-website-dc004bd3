A nonprofit director called me last quarter. Her board had passed a motion. The org needed to "use AI to be more efficient." She had ninety days to come back with a plan.

She asked me which tool she should buy.

I told her not to buy anything yet. Not because the tools are bad. Because the tool was the wrong question.

That conversation has shaped almost every conversation I have had with nonprofit leaders since. The board motion she described is now happening at almost every nonprofit I work with, and every CSR team, and every foundation officer trying to decide what to fund next. The pattern is identical: a top-down mandate to "use AI", a tool-shopping reaction, and a team that gets handed something they don't trust and don't know how to maintain.

The fix is not a smarter tool. The fix is a framework that starts with what the work is actually doing for the people you serve, then decides where AI fits, and where it shouldn't go.

That framework is ADAPT. We teach it on the personal brand and we deliver it through Impact Loop. Five phases, in a fixed order. Skip a phase and the work breaks in a predictable way.

Here is what each phase looks like when the audience is a nonprofit, foundation, or impact-led organization, and why the order matters more for mission-driven work than for any other kind.

## Why nonprofits are different

A SaaS company can adopt AI badly and recover by next quarter. The customer doesn't see most of the work. The contract renewal will tell them whether the operational change actually mattered.

Nonprofits operate on a different substrate. Trust. Built over years of consistent, recognizably human communication with donors, foundations, board members, beneficiaries, and the community. Erode that trust and there is no quarterly contract renewal that fixes it. The donor who feels like your appeal was AI-written stops opening your emails. The foundation officer who reads your grant report and thinks "this sounds like every other report" funds someone else. The community member who senses a generic comms voice on your landing page senses generic comms everywhere else too.

This is the part most generic AI adoption playbooks miss. They optimize for speed and efficiency, which is fine when speed and efficiency are the buyer's metric. For nonprofits, the metric is trust per dollar of comms spend. AI accelerates whatever is already there, and if what is already there is generic, AI generates more generic faster.

The ADAPT framework was designed around this constraint. Assess your real work. Discover where AI genuinely fits. Apply small. Produce honestly. Transform what works into a system the team can run without you.

Five phases. Each one with a specific job for nonprofit work.

## Assess: what is this specific piece of work actually doing?

The Assess phase asks a question every nonprofit team should be able to answer for every recurring piece of communication they produce. What is this specific piece of work doing for the people we serve?

A donor appeal email, a grant report, a board memo, a stakeholder update, a community newsletter, a campaign film, a website redesign, each of these has a different job. The reader is bringing a different question. The trust currency is different.

Most nonprofits skip this question and treat all communications as one bucket called "comms." Then someone says "we should use AI for comms" and the team starts shopping for tools that produce comms output. The output looks fine, the team feels efficient, and three months later donations are flatter than they were before.

The Assess phase fixes this by separating the work into three jobs:

**Operational.** Internal docs, FAQ pages, meeting notes, contractor briefs, process documentation. The reader doesn't care who wrote it. They care that it is accurate and clear. AI is good at this. Often better than the human who would otherwise rush it. Use AI here without hesitation.

**Informational.** Blog posts that explain a program, how-to guides for community members, summaries of research, technical breakdowns of a service. The reader cares the information is right and the explanation is clear. They mostly do not care whether you specifically wrote it. AI can do most of the work, but the team should rewrite the voice-heavy parts in their own words. Use AI as a co-writer, not as the writer.

**Relational.** Donor appeals. Foundation grant reports. The story of why your org exists. Sensitive community communications. Anything where the reader is paying attention to whether the voice still sounds like a person who cares. AI cannot do this work. Not because the model isn't smart enough. Because the reader can tell, and the second they can tell, the trust is gone.

A nonprofit team that runs Assess properly leaves the phase with a list of every recurring communication, sorted into one of these three buckets. That list is the foundation for everything else. AI never gets used for relational work without a deliberate exception. AI gets used for operational and informational work freely, with appropriate review.

This sounds simple. It is not what most nonprofits are doing right now. Most nonprofits are using AI on relational work because that is where the team feels most overwhelmed, and that is exactly the work AI cannot help with.

## Discover: where does AI actually fit?

Once the work is sorted, the Discover phase looks at the operational and informational categories and asks where AI would genuinely help.

Genuinely is the operative word. Plenty of AI tools can technically do plenty of nonprofit work. The Discover question is narrower: where would AI free up time, energy, or capacity that the team is currently losing, without introducing a fragility that costs more than the time saved?

A few patterns we see consistently:

**AI fits well for the document avalanche.** Grant agreements, contracts, policy docs, donor stewardship plans, program reports, most of these have predictable structure. AI is good at first drafts, summary extraction, and consistency checks. A small AI workflow to summarize incoming foundation reports for the executive director can save 4–6 hours a week and never touches the relational layer.

**AI fits well for inbox triage.** A nonprofit's email inbox is one of the highest-volume, most-distracted places in the operation. An AI agent that classifies incoming email, donor inquiry, grant deadline, vendor invoice, internal, and routes it to the right place gives the leadership team back focus without ever drafting an email that goes out under their name.

**AI fits well for the program-data chase.** Pulling stats out of CRM exports, M&E reports, attendance trackers. Mostly classification and extraction work. Deterministic-shaped, low-risk.

**AI does not fit for the donor relationship layer.** Personal thank-you notes, the appeal email that opens the year-end campaign, the story of a beneficiary on the homepage. These are the moments where the donor is deciding whether to stay with you. The voice has to be yours.

**AI does not fit for sensitive community work.** Communications to youth in the program, conversations with families in crisis, anything in the community-organizer lane. The risk of an AI tone-deafness incident is much higher than the time savings.

The Discover phase ends with one specific outcome to build toward. Not five. Not a department-wide rollout. One workflow, one team member, one specific use. The goal is to leave Discover knowing exactly what you are about to apply AI to and exactly what you will measure.

## Apply: build the smallest version that could work

Most nonprofits hear "AI strategy" and start writing a six-month roadmap. The Apply phase pushes back hard. Build the smallest version that could possibly produce the outcome. Use the simplest tool that could possibly work. Sometimes that's an off-the-shelf AI tool used by one person on one workflow. Sometimes it's a Python script. Sometimes it's a written process before any AI is involved at all.

The point is not sophistication. The point is signal.

A nonprofit team that runs Apply correctly has a working version of one specific AI workflow within two weeks. The version is small enough to debug. The version is small enough to throw away if it doesn't work. Most importantly, the version is being used by one team member who reports back honestly on whether it actually saves time.

Two failure modes we see at this phase:

**The committee builds the system.** Five people in three meetings argue about what the AI should do. Nothing ships. Three months later the org has spent on consulting and has zero working AI. Apply is owned by one person.

**The vendor builds the system.** A SaaS tool gets bought. Onboarding takes three weeks. Customization eats two more. The tool produces output the team doesn't trust and quietly stops getting used. Apply uses the simplest tool that could possibly work, which is almost never the most-marketed tool in the category.

The deliverable from Apply is a working v1 that one person on the team is actively using. Not a deck. Not a strategy memo. A working version.

## Produce: compare with-AI to without-AI honestly

Apply produces a v1. Produce evaluates it.

Most nonprofits skip this step. The v1 produces something, the team feels productive, and the comparison to "what would have happened without the AI" never gets made. Three months later nobody can answer whether the AI is actually saving time, and the v1 is treated as a permanent part of the workflow because removing it would feel like going backwards.

The Produce phase forces three measurements over a defined trial period (we use 4 weeks):

**Time.** How long does this workflow take with AI, end-to-end including review? How long did it take without AI? Be honest about the review time. AI that saves 30 minutes of drafting but adds 45 minutes of review is a net loss.

**Quality.** Is the output as good as what the team was producing manually? Better? Worse? This is where you also run the Trust Test. Take a sample of the output, hand it to someone who is in your audience (a donor, a board member, a community partner) without telling them how it was produced, and ask whether anything reads off. If they spot the AI, the output is not ready for relational work.

**Team energy.** Does the team feel more capable or more drained? AI workflows that technically save time but burn out the operator are a net loss. The energy metric is the one most boards forget to ask about.

A v1 that passes all three measurements graduates to Transform. A v1 that fails any of them gets either redesigned or retired. Not all v1s graduate. That is by design, Produce is the gate that prevents the org from accumulating AI workflows nobody trusts.

## Transform: hand off so the team can run without you

The fifth phase is the one that compounds.

A v1 that has passed Produce is converted into a system the team can run without the consultant, without the technical person, without the original builder. The transformation outputs are simple but specific:

**A runbook.** Step-by-step documentation of the workflow. If a new team member could not run the workflow from this document, the runbook is not done.

**A maintenance plan.** What happens when the AI tool changes pricing, the prompt stops working, the API key rotates, or the team member who built it leaves. A maintenance plan is one page. Most nonprofits skip this and end up with abandoned AI workflows when the original builder moves on.

**A tone-of-voice gate.** Specifically for any workflow that touches relational or near-relational work. A documented checklist of what the output must sound like before it gets sent. This is what prevents drift over time, where the AI's voice slowly leaks into communications that should sound like the org's voice.

**A budget.** What does this workflow cost per month in tool fees, API calls, and team time? Most nonprofits start with the budget question never answered, then find out six months in that the AI experiment has cost more than the time it saved.

When all four are in place, the v1 is officially transformed into a system the team owns. That is the end of the cycle.

## What this means for foundations and CSR teams

Most foundations are now writing checks for "AI literacy" or "digital transformation" grants. The pattern most grantees are running with that money is: hire a consultant, buy tools, run pilots that don't get measured, write a year-end report that uses the word "innovation."

A foundation that wants its money to compound should fund the ADAPT cycle, not the tool stack. Specifically:

Fund the Assess and Discover phases first. A grantee that comes back at the 90-day mark with a clear picture of where AI fits and where it doesn't is in a stronger position than a grantee that has shipped five tools nobody uses.

Fund the Transform phase explicitly. The runbook, the maintenance plan, the tone-of-voice gate, the budget. This is where the ROI of AI grants actually compounds. Without it, every grant cycle is starting over.

Avoid funding generic "AI training" without an associated cycle. Workshop-shaped training has near-zero retention without an immediate Apply phase tied to the team's real work.

For CSR teams: the same pattern applies. The corporate-volunteer day at the nonprofit is rarely the highest-leverage AI contribution. Funding one team member at a partner nonprofit to run a 4-week ADAPT cycle, with an experienced trainer, produces a working system the org owns. That's a more durable corporate-impact story than a one-day hackathon.

## A closing note for nonprofit leaders staring at the board motion

The pressure to "use AI" is real. The board motion is not going away. But the response that actually serves your work is not to buy the most-marketed tool and roll it out across the org. The response is to slow down for two weeks, run the Assess and Discover phases honestly, and then build one small thing that does one specific job for one specific team member.

That sounds slower than the alternatives. It is significantly faster on the timescale that matters, the timescale of trust, retention, and durable communications capacity. The orgs that get this right end up with AI workflows that are quietly running their operational and informational work, while their relational work stays unmistakably theirs.
